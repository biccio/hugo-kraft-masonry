I have started and abandoned more side projects than I care to count. The pattern was always the same: enthusiasm, a burst of progress, a missed week, guilt, abandonment. The 20-minute rule broke the cycle.

<!--more-->

The rule is exactly what it sounds like. Each day, if I want to work on a side project, I commit to twenty minutes. Not two hours on weekends. Not "when I have a solid block of time". Twenty minutes, any time, any day.

The logic behind it is not motivational — it is structural. Two hours requires clearing a calendar, achieving a mental state, eliminating distractions. Twenty minutes requires almost nothing. You can do twenty minutes before a meeting, between tasks, on a lunch break, while something is compiling. The activation energy is low enough that "I don't have time today" stops being a plausible excuse.

What surprised me was how often twenty minutes turned into an hour. Not because I pushed myself to continue, but because once I was actually working on something I cared about, the timer became irrelevant. The rule gets you past the start, which turns out to be the hard part.

The second effect was pace. Projects that would previously have stalled for weeks now crept forward every day. Slow, yes — but the direction was always forward. This theme is the product of about forty sessions of twenty minutes or more, spread across two months. Without the rule, it would have been abandoned after the third weekend.

Set a timer. Twenty minutes. Start.
