My phone has seventeen note-taking apps installed. I use none of them. I use a gridded A5 notebook and a fountain pen that cost eleven euros and has been refilled so many times the barrel is permanently stained with blue-black ink.

<!--more-->

The argument for paper is not nostalgia, or at least not entirely. It is that the friction is the feature. When writing costs something — even just the small effort of uncapping a pen and opening a physical object — the things you choose to write down are different. More considered, maybe. Or at least more deliberate.

I keep a daily page: tasks on the left, observations on the right, a small sketch of whatever I looked at for more than thirty seconds that day. A cloud formation over the cathedral, the arrangement of cups on a café table, the way a particular street corner looks in November rain. Not because I am any kind of artist, but because drawing something forces you to actually see it rather than just notice it.

The notebook also serves as a record of what I was thinking — not just what I did. Calendars track events; notebooks track the texture of ordinary days. I have notebooks going back a decade and they are, collectively, a more accurate account of who I was than anything I posted online during the same period.
